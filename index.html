Labyrinth tactics

Turn-based multiplayer tactics game with grid-based movement, set in 1920s Egypt. The inspiration to create this game came from a desire to play an online board game that offers unique advantages due to its digital nature. This allows for the use of mechanics that cannot exist in a physical board game. This includes features such as hiding other players' characters while only showing yours, randomizing map generation with hidden secrets, limiting visibility to only a small area around the characters, and concealing enemy NPC movements.

This is currently my largest ongoing personal project.

Notable coding features:

  • Procedural generated arena: It was an important aspect to create more than just a typical labyrinth; I aimed for wider spaces and a more varied layout. The algorithm goes through multiple loops after the initial generation to accomplish several tasks, including filling closed-up spaces, refining corners, adjusting wall heights based on neighboring walls, and proportionally scattering the spawn of entities. This can result in random and interesting layouts.
  • Grid based movement: The movement occurs at an abstract level initially within a matrix representing the map, which stores information about each grid tile. If the move is permitted based on this, the character can move physically to the desired location.
  • Visibility calculations: Each character only has a limited vision range around them, and that vision is also blocked by walls. It was implemented using SphereCast to check the objects around the character after each move and drawing a LineCast between them and the player to check if they are blocked by wall or not. If an object is in the sphere and not blocked, then it becomes visible and interactable to the player.
  • Round system: Each player has a limited amount of time taking steps with their character and doing a selected action. If they end their turn, it jumps to the next player sequentially, and after every full round, the enemies (Mummies) move.
  • Multiple character control: Each player can control multiple characters simultanously by switching between them during their turn.
  • Treasure map: Players have a treasure map, where they can spend points to reveal tiles of the map to see if there are any treasures around that location. Similar to Minesweeper.
  • Enemy AI
  • Multiplayer aspect: The game supports hosting a lobby locally up to 6 players and up to 4 characters per player. The actions and events are synchronised between the players. There is also an ingame ranking to show who is leading with points during the game.

The next stage for the game is to add more characters to the base roster with their custom mechanics, and complete the remaining networking features.

Powerpuff Sidescroller fan-game (Released on itch.io)

It is a small-scale casual web browser fan-game (works on mobile and PC), where the goal is to reach a highscore while flying through infinitely generating map sections. The game features a layered, dynamic background composition, sequenced 2D animations, collectible coins and scoring system, character switching, health recovery system, gameplay settings, polished movement controls, online leaderboard, unlockable items.
I recently released this game, and it managed to achieve great popularity beyond my expectations with daily 4-5k plays even weeks after the release.

The game is available to play here: Itch.io

Background and release

  • The game was made based on a concept seen in a music video as a non-profit fan-game.

    • [Screenshot from the MV]
  • The development process, from inception to release, took 1.5 months, which I dedicated my free time to. I shared the progress steps on social media, the compilation can be seen above.
  • After running a beta test with helpful participants, I successfully launched the game on itch.io and received overwhelmingly positive feedback, for which I'm very proud.
  • After release, I started collaborating with an artist who offered his help in creating more assets.

Programming points:

  • Endless map generation where map tiles, composed as prefabs, and world effects (SFX & VFX) are instantiated at runtime and subsequently destroyed when they leave the viewport to not overload the memory.
  • Smooth character movement with mobile friendly input (handles both PC and mobile controls).
  • The character movement is restricted to a fixed vertical line with upper and lower boundaries. When the player left-clicks or taps on the screen, the character will smoothly interpolate (lerp) to the height of the selected point.
  • Persistent saving system using json files with custom in-built versioning that handles the problem with itch.io not recognizing PlayerPrefs in different WebGL builds.
  • Character switching mechanics for which I used ScriptableObjects to store character data.
  • For version control, I'm using separate DEV and Live branches to be able to release bug fix patches for released versions while also working on new features.

Tower stacking casual game (Realeased on itch.io)

Physics based tower building fan-game, consisting of a large set of different figures to place and balance on top of each other. The inspiration came from this game: YouTube. Published for WebGL and Android.
Link to play: Itch.io

Features:

  • To make a tile land and remain stable, I implemented a system where if the tile's velocity becomes close to zero, its rigidbody type switches to static, effectively freezing its transforms. Achieving the right balance required a significant amount of time fine-tuning the physics values. To achieve zero velocity, the player must carefully position and rotate the tile so that it remains stable upon landing.
  • The highscore is determined by calculating the highest global point of the last successfully placed sprite asset, based on the y extents of its bounding box from the sprite renderer.
  • I made the game easily extendable, with a seamless pipeline for adding any number of additional tiles.

JRPG battle prototype

The goal of this project was to recreate a classic turn-based JRPG battle system.

Includes:

  • Heavy use of OOP and abstraction for expandability (Characters, Enemies, Items, Abilities, Effects). Each character and enemy has attributes (Health, MP, Initiation, Luck, etc...), each item and ability has properties like damage, cost, stat gain, and they can effect multiple targets in some instances.
  • Queue system
    • Each character has an initiation attribute that effects their ranking in the queue. I created a system that calculates the turn order of the active characters based on these attributes and refreshes when the player switches characters or when someone dies.
  • Character switching
    • There is an expandable cast of characters, but only 4 can be present on the battlefield at a time. The character in turn can be swapped to a inactive character who takes their place on the field, and updates every necessary attribute.

Chatting simulator with custom UDL

In this chatting simulator the player can engage in realistic conversations with a virtual partner, choosing from preset dialogues and answer options. The dialogues can be written and customized by anyone using a user-defined language (UDL) that I created specifically for this game. This system allows creating branching storylines and realistic chat experiences, and has the flexibility and programmability to create intricate conversations. What I was aiming for with this project was to enable users to create their own self-made chat partner dialogues with the use of the UDL, and build a public community catalogue of virtual chat profiles that people can access and can contribute to.

Features:

  • Chat saving and restoring
  • Realistic animations and timings
  • Dialogue and picture upload support
    • Dialogues and pictures can be conveniently added for the virtual partners directly from the game menu. These entries are then organized and attributed to the corresponding partner under their respective names.
    • The game supports reading from multiple text files, so it's also possible to slice up the dialogues for better comprehension and clarity.

Constellation generator

This was a collaborative project in a team of two to create a constellation generator. It heavily relies on math calculations. The background was created using shadergraph, the project was developed in Unity.

Algorithm breakdown:

    1. Generating the stars (Complexity: Moderate)
    • Generating stars and ensuring they are placed at a minimum distance from each other.
    • For each star, it generates random X and Y coordinates within certain ranges (1000 to 4000) while avoiding collisions with existing stars.
    2. Calculating the closest stars (Complexity: Moderate to High)
    • Finds the closest star to a given star within the constellation based on distance.
    • It keeps track of the closest and second closest stars based on the chosen algorithm. (The second closest star is particularly significant because, in the case where A's closest star is B, it will always mean that conversely B's closest star is A. However, this principle does not apply to the second closest stars.)
    • The function may mark stars as "used" to avoid selecting them again.
    3. Drawing lines between the stars (Complexity: Moderate)
    • Calculates and sets the position and rotation of a line connecting two stars by computing the angle between the two stars' positions.
    • calculates the length of the line based on the distance between the stars and adjusts its size.
    4. Collision handling (Complexity: High)
    • Collisions can occur between lines or between a line and a star.
    • The function attempts to redraw the line to avoid collisions, ensuring that lines connect to stars without intersecting with other lines.
    • The exact logic for redrawing depends on the specific use case and conditions. It may involve finding a different target star or adjusting the line's position and rotation.
    • The difficulty with fixing the lines was ensuring there are no stars ending up with no lines, and the line count shouldn't exceed the star count.
    5. Centering the figure (Complexity: Moderate)
    • To center the constellation figure within the display area.
    • Finds the minimum and maximum coordinates among all stars to determine the constellation's width and height.
    • Then, it computes the X and Y offsets needed to place the figure in the center of the screen.
